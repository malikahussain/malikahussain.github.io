  <h1 style="margin-bottom: 30px; font-size: 2em; color: #333;">About Me</h1>

  <p style="font-size: 1.1em; line-height: 1.8; margin-bottom: 30px;">
    I am a Postdoctoral Fellow at the <strong>Knee Regulatory Research Center</strong> in the
    <strong>John Chambers College of Business and Economics</strong> at
    <strong>West Virginia University</strong>. I received my Ph.D. in Economics from
    <strong>George Mason University</strong> in 2025.
  </p>

  <h2 style="margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 20px; font-size: 1.5em; color: #444;">Research Interests</h2>
  <p style="font-size: 1.05em; line-height: 1.8; margin-bottom: 30px;">
    My research focuses on <strong>applied microeconomics</strong>, with particular emphasis on
    <strong>historical political economy</strong>, <strong>development economics</strong>, and
    <strong>public health</strong>. I study how political and cultural institutions shaped vaccination
    campaigns and mortality patterns in colonial India, and how these historical legacies continue
    to influence contemporary outcomes.
  </p>

  <h2 style="margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 20px; font-size: 1.5em; color: #444;">Current Research</h2>
  <p style="font-size: 1.05em; line-height: 1.8; margin-bottom: 30px;">
    My job market paper examines the cultural and religious roots of vaccine hesitancy in colonial India,
    exploring how Hindu religious beliefs affected smallpox vaccination uptake and its long-term societal
    impacts. I use newly compiled historical data and spatial econometric methods to understand the
    mechanisms behind health behavior and policy effectiveness.
  </p>
