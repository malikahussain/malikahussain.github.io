Full abstract

This paper presents new historical evidence on the cultural roots of vaccine hesitancy and its medium-term effects. Using a newly compiled district-level dataset with religion-specific vaccination rates across colonial India (1868–1878), I examine how Hindu religious beliefs affected smallpox vaccination uptake. An instrumental variables approach using historical Hindu temple desecration sites shows that a one standard deviation increase in Hindu population share leads to a 0.89 to 1.98 percentage points decrease in Hindu vaccination rate at the district level. These effects are specific to Hindu populations and operate through religious and cultural practices. By 1891, a one standard deviation increase in Hindu share is associated with 17.7 and 12.6 percentage point increases in unmarried and widow ratios among young adults aged 10–25. The findings highlight the societal impact of culturally-driven vaccine hesitancy and guide culturally sensitive health policies.

“Mistrust and Vaccination in Colonial India: 1870–1926” with Noel D. Johnson [Slides] [Research Remix Podcast (Noel Johnson)]
We examine how the 1857 Indian Rebellion shaped long-run mistrust of British colonial health interventions and its effects on smallpox vaccination campaigns across India from 1870 to 1926.

“Railways and Health Outcomes in Colonial India” [SSRN]
I study the causal effect of railway expansion on health outcomes in colonial India, exploiting the staggered rollout of the railway network as a quasi-experiment to identify impacts on disease spread and mortality.

“Colonization in a Network of Allies” with Benhur Ruqsana [SSRN]
We model how colonial powers exploited pre-existing political networks of allied states, showing that network position determined the sequencing and terms of colonial incorporation across pre-colonial India.

Selected Published Papers

“Flushed with Success: How Participation in Social Schemes Influences Toilet Adoption in Rural India” with M. Tyagi, D. Kumar, and Md T. Khan. Journal of Biosocial Science, February 2025. [Published Article]
Using data from rural India, we show that participation in government social schemes significantly increases household toilet adoption, with effects operating through both income and norm-change channels.

“Political Connections of Firms and Access to Credit: Evidence from the Indian Corporate Sector” with M. Tyagi. Structural Change and Economic Dynamics, 2024. [Published Article]
We document that politically connected firms in India enjoy preferential access to credit, with connected firms receiving larger loans at lower rates, particularly from public sector banks.

“Political Connections and Access to Credit: Evidence from Micro-enterprises in India” with M. Tyagi. Small Business Economics, 2024. [Published Article]
We show that micro-enterprise owners with political connections in India are significantly more likely to receive formal credit, with effects concentrated in regions with weaker financial development.
